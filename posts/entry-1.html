Seven Days Late

Nearly three in the morning, three hours before training, and the first negotiation has already started.

I am already late.

That is the first honest line.

This was supposed to start seven days ago. The site was ready enough. The idea was clear enough. The decision had already been made. And still I waited.

Not because I needed more clarity.

Because delay is comfortable when it is dressed as preparation.

Now it is nearly three in the morning. In three hours I am supposed to get up and train. I can already feel the negotiation starting. It is soft. Reasonable. Almost intelligent.

You slept too little.
You need to get through the day.
You can train tomorrow.
Do not make this harder than it has to be.

That voice is not completely wrong. That is what makes it dangerous.

I probably will be tired. The day probably will be harder. Work will not care that I slept badly. My body will not suddenly become heroic because I wrote a manifesto on the internet. There will be consequences.

But there are always consequences.

There is the consequence of getting up tired.

And there is the consequence of proving, again, that the first uncomfortable moment is enough to make me fold.

That second consequence is worse.

I keep wanting a clean beginning. A cinematic first day. Full sleep. Clear head. Strong workout. Perfect entry. Some opening scene that makes the whole thing feel legitimate.

Instead I get this.

Seven days late.
Three hours of sleep available.
A body already looking for a way out.
A mind trying to turn fear into strategy.

Fine.

This is probably the correct beginning.

Not inspiring. Not impressive. Not optimized. Just the exact place where the pattern usually wins.

The fear is simple: if I get up, train, and push through the day, I might suffer.

The deeper fear is worse: if I get up, train, and push through the day, I lose one of my favorite excuses.

I do not need to destroy myself tomorrow. I do not need to pretend sleep does not matter. I do not need to turn stupidity into discipline.

But I do need to stop using imperfect conditions as a permission slip.

So the rule is small and brutal.

When the alarm goes, I get up.
I put my feet on the floor.
I train at whatever level is available.
I do not dramatize it.
I do not negotiate with the pillow.
I do not call surrender recovery.

If the workout is weak, it is weak.
If the day is hard, it is hard.
If I pay for tonight, I pay.

But I will not pay twice by also becoming the kind of person who quits before the day begins.

This record starts late.

Good.

Now there is no clean image to protect.

Only the next alarm.
